Mitigation Guidelines

Mitigation efforts for landslides in areas with moderate, high, and very high vulnerability classes:

  • Avoid creating new rice paddies or ponds on steep slopes, as this can cause the soil to move easily.
  • Avoid cutting down trees around slopes.
  • Avoid building settlements in landslide-prone areas, especially in lower slope areas or on the edge of steep river valleys.
  • Cover cracks on slopes to prevent rapid water ingress.
  • Reduce slope compactness by terracing land in slope areas.
  • Maintain slope drainage properly to prevent water from flowing out of the slope.
  • Build retaining structures with strong foundations to prevent soil movement that can cause landslides.
  • Plant trees with deep roots. Ensure that the planting distance is not too close. The space between trees is interspersed with short plants that can maintain water drainage.
  • Avoid excavation in the lower parts of steep slopes, as this can disturb slope stability.
  • Follow information and instructions from the government regarding the possibility of landslides.

Meanwhile, mitigation efforts that can be undertaken by the government include:

  • Developing early warning systems in landslide-prone areas.
  • Disseminating information and socializing landslide disasters to the public through various media and methods, both formally and informally.
  • Coordinating with local governments and related regional organizations in efforts to reduce the risk of landslides.
  • Formulating and explaining programs or policies related to landslide mitigation activities.
  • Monitoring and monitoring ground movements, assisted by EWS (Early Warning System) or manually assisted by community volunteers.
  • Providing facilities and infrastructure, especially heavy equipment and equipment to facilitate landslide disaster mitigation.
  • Designing and recommending activity programs to reduce the impact of landslide disaster risks.
  • Facilitating services to the community to facilitate the dissemination of updated landslide disaster-related information.
  • Relocation of landslide-prone areas as an effort to reduce the risk of landslides.

Data sources:

  • Monthly rainfall data for the year 2022 at the Sleman Geophysics Station, Yogyakarta Climatology Station, and Yogyakarta Meteorology Station from BMKG(https://dataonline.bmkg.go.id/)
  • Monthly rainfall data for the year 2022 from the "Climate Hazards Center InfraRed Precipitation with Station data (CHIRPS)" dataset - Climate Hazards Center - UC Santa Barbara (https://chc.ucsb.edu/data/chirps)
  • Rock type data from the Geological Map of Indonesia - The Ministry of Energy and Mineral Resources of The Republic of Indonesia (https://geologi.esdm.go.id/geomap)
  • Soil Type Map Data of Bantul Regency - Regional Development Planning Agency of Bantul Regency (https://bappeda.bantulkab.go.id/index.php/layanan-informasi/formulir-permintaan-informasi )
  • Land use and land cover data from the Land and Spatial Planning Office of Bantul Regency (https://geoportal.bantulkab.go.id)
  • DEM data from the Geospatial Information Agency (https://tanahair.indonesia.go.id/demnas)
  • Landslide incident data for the years 2016 - 2020 from the Regional Disaster Management Agency of Bantul Regency

Data Processing

Rainfall data from BMKG is in xlsx format, containing monthly rainfall values at specific observation station points. Meanwhile, CHIRPS data is in raster .bil format, containing monthly rainfall DN values worldwide. This CHIRPS data is converted into a set of coordinate points with rainfall values. Then, the converted CHIRPS data is combined with the BMKG data, followed by IDW interpolation to obtain annual rainfall data in shp polygon format classified into five intensity classes.

DEM data is in raster .tif format, containing DN elevation values. The DEM data is processed to obtain slope data.

Meanwhile, rock type, land cover, and soil type data are in shp polygon format.
